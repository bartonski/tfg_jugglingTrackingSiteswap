Introduction 

1.1. Motivation 

Siteswap     [1]   is the most widespread notation to describe any pattern of 

juggling, and although it is true that sometimes there are tricks that can be named with 
words, these change depending on the country or even the juggler himself, so that 
the  siteswap  is also the only existing notation sufficiently widespread 
to be able to assume that it can be produce a technical communication with any other 
juggler. 

As a notation, it has the advantage that it mathematically defines the sequence of 

releases that follow the pattern, so that there are already applications (and they are quite 
widespread within the community)  [2]  that, based on the  siteswap,  generate simulations of 
the patterns, allowing jugglers to study them to learn how to perform them, 
investigate new tricks by obtain sequences that are mathematically correct, or 
even theorize about new trends within juggling with the simulation of 
tricks that are initially merely theoretical. 

In any case, there is no accessible application capable of, from the video 

of a juggler, extract the  siteswap  you are making. This could be quite 
useful for the community, since at an international level there is a large volume of 
publications on social networks and general exchange of audiovisual content about 
juggling that in many cases are not accompanied by the  siteswap itself , which 
makes the process of power difficult. imitate certain patterns that may be more attractive. 

This work focuses on developing precisely this idea and creating a system 

capable of both tracking the juggler's balls and extracting the 
necessary information from that tracking to calculate the   corresponding  siteswap .

Ideally, the system should be able to classify any video from one 

or more jugglers with all the   siteswaps   that are made, which may in turn be 
siteswaps  that include variations and synchronous launches. However, the 
real proposed scope (can be covered within the framework of a TFG) is that in the input video 
there is only one juggler who performs a single siteswap with all asynchronous launches 
(only one object is launched at a time), internal ( the trajectory is from inside to outside) 
and without variations (there are no behind-the-back launches or similar), so that 
the system only has to calculate a  siteswap  that is as clear as possible.

2.1.2. Origin 

The invention of   siteswap   [4]   is attributed to Paul Klimek from California in 1981, 

although at that time it was called “ Quantum Juggling ” due to an analogy 
he made between atoms with their nuclei surrounded by clouds of electrons that went up and 
down as if they were a juggler with their balls. 

Paul Klimek realized that by looking at a juggler from the side without paying attention 

in his hands he was able to observe cycles. Working on that idea he added the time of 
flight to the time of holding the ball in his hand and realized that it was an integral multiple of the 
time between throws  [5].  From there he developed the notation we know now. 

In any case, Paul was not the only one to make this discovery. In some 

Just before 1986, Paul told a friend of his “ Quantum Juggling ” notation, 
and he put him in contact with Don Hatch, as he had discovered something similar. 
Indeed they both used and understood the same system, and Don Hatch 
had also discovered it around 1981  [6]. 

Also independently, in 1985 in Cambridge, Mike Day, Colin Wright and 

Adam Chalcraft arrived at a very similar system, except that they considered all the  figures to have one less value, although probably if they had continued with their system
for longer  without knowing about “ Quantum Juggling ” they would have added that value to be able  to represent the gaps and make them fulfilled. certain mathematical principles  [7].

 

About those years, Bruce Tiemann, an American juggler, discovered a system 

to generate tricks called  siteswap  (which had nothing to do with the notation 
we know today), which was based on the concept that each different permutation of 
numbers implies a different juggling trick for each possible number of objects. 
He used this concept to generate new tricks by changing sites in these 
permutations, hence   site - swap   [8].   It did not serve as notation, although Bruce had 
his own system of writing tricks based on the spins of the clubs and whether 
or not the throws changed hands [7]. 

In 1986 Paul and Bruce met and exchanged their discoveries. Paul leaves 

Bruce had to explain his system first, to make sure that what he told him was not 
influenced by his own discoveries [5].   Once Bruce told him his way of 
generating tricks, Paul proceeded to tell him his way of notating them, which for Bruce 
was a discovery, and he immediately considered it superior to his own notation  [7].

2.1.3. Items 

As mentioned above,  siteswaps  are alphanumeric strings 

which represent the juggling patterns and act as a score for the 
jugglers. Some examples could be “3”, “645” or “db97531”. 

In these sequences each character represents a throw, and its value 

represents the number of times it takes for that ball to be picked up again. If 
the character is a letter, it simply represents a number with more than one figure, 
since the alphabet used is {1,2,3,4,5,6,7,8,9,a,b ,CD,...}. Time is understood as 
the period between one throw and the next (from either hand). On a theoretical level 
all times measure the same, although on a practical level if you analyze the video 
of a juggler you can find small variations between the times (due 
to inaccuracies due to not being able to replicate perfect executions). 

The   siteswap   also presupposes that the launches are carried out alternating the 

hands. In this way, with R being the right hand and L being the left hand, all 
siteswaps 

are carried out following [R,L,R,L,R,L,R,L,…] (or any sequence in which R 

and L alternate). Although both theoretically and practically it is usually considered a 
notation for two hands, it is also valid for a larger number, so that if a 
juggler had three skilled hands (or used one foot and both hands, for example) the 
following could be followed: writing down the tricks as well. Another example is   passing   (juggling 
between several people), which for its notation uses an extension of the  Vanilla Siteswap 
with small extensions, but the same base. 

Thus, if we had 3 balls “x”, “y” and “z” and we wanted to make the cascade of 3 balls, 

First we would throw “x” with the right hand, then “y” with the left, then “z” with the 
right, then “x” with the left and so on. Writing the sequence 
becomes [x,y,z,x,y,z,x,y,z,...], and it can be seen that each ball is thrown 3 times 
after the last time it was thrown, that is, each ball makes a 3 in notation

representing the absence of balls in one hand. Thus, if in a pattern two balls are thrown

base systems, the one based on color masks. To this end, the approach followed to 
meet the objective of this submodule is to use the basic operation of 
Barton Chittinden's program   [18],   based on background subtraction, so it does not need the 
color information beforehand. The key idea of ​​this program is that
the contours of everything that has produced movement (to a greater or lesser extent) 
between different  frames are obtained  . The general structure of this submodule is to obtain the greatest 
number of detections corresponding to balls, see the average color they usually have, and 
return a certain range that contains it. The problem with using background subtraction for 
detections is that a large part of these do not correspond to the balls, but to other 
movements that may exist in the plane such as the arms and hands of the

juggler. However, it is important to note that it is not necessary to have all 
the ball detections, just try to ensure that there are no false positives. 
Therefore, each detection is subjected to a series of relatively strict filters that 
aim to avoid all detections that do not belong to balls, even at the cost of losing 
many detections that do. To implement these filters, 
auxiliary functions from   OpenCV   [38]   have been used to obtain the base information of each 
contour, so that the restrictions could then be created manually. 

These filters are: 

• Filtering by contour area: Contours that are too small 

we assume noise (probably small movements of the head or 
body). 

• A filtering by the number of sides of the polygon that most closely resembles the contour: 

For each contour, the approximate polygon that most closely resembles it is obtained, 
so that to consider the contour as valid it is required that this 
polygon have at least N sides (and consequently that it have an 
approximately circular shape). 

• That the polygon that most resembles be convex: Finally and with the same 

Finally, it is required that the polygon that most closely resembles be convex, so 
contours that are not relatively circular but that have been 
approximated by polygons with more than N sides are discarded. 

Once you have all the balls, the next step is to extract the color range. He 

The most repeated color is the one assumed to belong to the balls (this causes the limitation 
that all the balls have to be the same color). To return the range,

and the lower and upper limits that make up the interval are returned.

6.1.1. 

“ Sitewap Extraction” Module  

Although obtaining the sequence of launches according to the relative minimums 

The movement of each ball on the horizontal axis works within certain minimums, in 
this iteration a new approach to the procedure is proposed; Instead of looking at the 
movements of a single axis, look at both axes at the same time.  

With this idea, we seek to obtain a point that separates the video into quadrants, so 

so that the vertical line delimits the areas in which each hand moves and the 
horizontal line the areas in which a ball is either in one hand or is in the air. In case 
a ball crosses the vertical line in any direction and/or the horizontal line from 
bottom to top, we know that there has been a pitch. 

•   Obtaining separation point in quadrants 

To obtain this point, a program has been implemented that processes the video 

before the sequence is attempted, and returns only those coordinates. 

Conceptually, the point separates the image into quadrants, so that the two 

The upper ones have the areas in which the least movement occurs, the one on the bottom 
right contains the area in which the left arm usually moves, and the one on the bottom left contains 
the same, but with the movements of the right arm. For this reason, it has been decided 
to make an implementation that the first thing it does is go through the entire video applying 
background subtraction, and all the detections it makes (without filtering them in any way) 
are saved in a heat map. An example of the heat map obtained is 
shown in figure 6.1.

each column, obtaining an  array  that can be understood as the different values

column of pixels. This array is put through a smoothing process, and the 
lowest values ​​are removed (Corresponding to the sides where there are no detections). With this, 
the relative minimum that has a lowest value is sought, which in theory always 
corresponds to the central point of the pattern.

•   Obtain sequence 

To obtain the sequence, the goal of the implementation is to control each ball 

when it crosses each axis, and obtain them chronologically ordered. 

To do this, the algorithm looks at each ball separately, first checking 

place whether the release it is in has already been posted or not. To take into account 
when a ball is thrown again, it is set at the X coordinate, because when there is a 
change of direction in that coordinate it means that the ball has been picked up to 
be thrown again. 

With this, each launch is checked to see if it crosses the vertical axis (at any 

direction) or the horizontal (from bottom to top) established by the separation point in 
quadrants. The first time this condition occurs by launch, the 
launch is marked as verified and the ball identifier, the   frame   of the 
launch and the hand that produced it are saved (information obtained by checking the 
quadrant that has just been abandoned). 

At this point, if you get the list of identifiers sorted according to the  frame  in 

the launch occurs, the 
desired sequence of launches could be obtained. However, this approach has a fundamental problem, which is the 
impossibility of detecting 0's or 2's, since even in ideal situations they are the only 
throws that do not change quadrants.

To try to solve this situation, the entire sequence of launches is run through. 

checking which hand has done it. In case the 
same hand is repeated twice in a row, it is known that there has been an error at some point, so 
an id “-1” is entered in the sequence that will be processed later and the error is counted (this is 
also allows obtaining the error rate). 

Another problem is that it can happen that a ball crosses one of the axes without 

has actually been thrown (for example, if an even number is to be thrown, say a 
4, the hand gesture is to make a circle towards the inside of the pattern, so the ball 
in the hand can cross the vertical axis, but be thrown outwards, so 
it should not be taken into account as a throw that changes quadrant). To 
solve this, instead of counting the throws at the moment in which one 
of the axes is crossed, they are saved as “possible throws”, which become considered 
definitive throws when the ball has finally penetrated a quadrant above 
a certain threshold. 

•   Obtaining  siteswap 

The extraction part of the   siteswap   has also had to be modified, since 

Now you have to deal with the “-1” identifiers that you may receive from the previous 
submodule.  

Since each occurrence of one of these identifiers means that it has been lost 

a detection, we can assume that there has been a 0 or a 2 in that position (if it 
had been another number, the launch detection would have still failed for some other 
reason, so there is no “penalty” for assuming otherwise) . In this way, 
two are generated from this list of identifiers, one that understands that where there is -

1's is because 

There are 0's in the  siteswap sequence , and another that understands that it is because there are 2's. This 
leaves out possible  site swaps  where depending on the position there could be either of 
the two, but the cases where this occurs are such a low percentage that it has been 
considered negligible. 

With this, an attempt is made to obtain the  siteswap 

with the list with 2's (they are more common), and if 

If a mathematically correct result is found, it is returned, if not, it is tried with the 
list with 0's, which follows the same logic, and if it is not found either, 
“ NotFound” is returned.  This idea leaves a possible point of failure, which would be the case in which the pattern 
actually contains 0's but by first assuming that there are 2's, the  siteswap  obtained 
is technically correct, so it would be returned. In any case, it is a 
quite specific case that with the current design is not possible to control.

7.1.   Description 

For the final version of the system, a program has been created whose general idea is 

launch a parallel execution for each video you want to check. In turn, in 
each execution, the input is passed through a detection and  tracking submodule , then 
the  siteswap is extracted  with the quadrants submodule and, if the result does not 
seem to be conclusive, the the coordinates. With this, you 
can return that result or do a more exhaustive evaluation in which 
MOTA, MOTP and general presence are calculated. Once all the executions are completed, the 
program returns a table per terminal with all the results. 

The program has a file in which you can establish the following 

settings: 

• Basic information on directories and name formats used by the program. 

• List of  siteswaps  (videos) to check with the program. 

• Detection and  tracking submodule  to be used. 

• A  flag  that marks whether you want to do all the  tracking  or directly load the 

results from existing files. 

• Color range in case you want to enter it manually for  tracking  by 

color masks. 

• A  flag  that indicates that instead of entering the color range manually, you want 

obtain by preprocessing. 

• The failure rate that is allowed to try to obtain the   siteswap   by 

quadrants before moving to coordinates. 

• A factor that marks the maximum length that the result is allowed to process 

to obtain the  siteswap  by quadrants before moving to coordinates.

• The number of indices used to check that a sequence repeats itself 

before returning it as  siteswap . 

• A  flag  that marks if you want to do the complete evaluation. 

• The number of decimal places displayed.

Conclusions and future work 

8.1.   Conclusions 

As mentioned in the previous section, the final result obtained is that, 

For videos recorded under ideal conditions, the system works well 77.27% of 
the time. Taking this into account, and despite the fact that the   dataset   used is so 
representative that most experienced jugglers will surely be 
able to identify   siteswaps   at sight, results of this level could be useful 
for people new to the subject, or even for the rest of the community in 
case they were maintained for videos in worse conditions. 

That is why future revisions of this work could add certain improvements. 

to work in less ideal cases, potentially leading to the launch of a widely used application  within the juggling niche (just like siteswap
simulators  ),  since there is an increasing number of technical jugglers raising the bar than  previously believed. possible, and - for the moment - even executing   9-ball  siteswaps with a period greater than 3  [40],  which are no longer easily identifiable by the majority of  experienced jugglers. 



In any case, the initially proposed objectives have been met, and 

Although we have not entered into solutions based on machine learning because they 
were beyond the initial approach of the work because they required a much more extensive process of defining, 
obtaining and annotating the  dataset  , the already existing   ball
detection and   tracking systems have been completely improved.  of juggling, which until 
now did not place special emphasis on maintaining the identities of the balls, and in addition  all the functionality related to the extraction of sequences and obtaining the siteswap
has been added  , being the first project of its kind accessible on the Internet  [41]. .
 

8.2.   Future work 

From the very approach of this project, two main ways of 

improvement: 

• Make a system with these characteristics that can also detect rings, 

juggling clubs and any other type of object. 

 
 
 

 

     52                           

Obtaining “ siteswap ” from juggling through video analysis 

 

• Make it so that, in addition to being able to obtain   vanilla siteswaps , it can detect 

patterns that require more complex notations such as   extended 
siteswap . 

In any case, it is interesting to note that for both extensions (one 

referring to the detection, and the other to the extraction of the  siteswap ), you can replace some 
module of the currently implemented system and use the rest. 

In any case, during the development and implementation of the different modules, 

More avenues for improvement have emerged that could be explored in the future. In addition to making 
modules that, maintaining the approach of this work, improve results, it could 
be interesting to test developments based on machine learning, which 
could very predictably improve all the current modules, requiring a high 
effort to expand the  dataset . Furthermore, even maintaining the current point of view 
both in scope of the system and in implementation, the possibility of 
implementing the post-processing module remains open, and, taking into account that the 
detection and  tracking module  that has given the best results requires the number of 
balls and their color range, another future avenue of work could be to eliminate this need, or 
be able to provide it automatically and efficiently without affecting the results 
obtained.

A.1.   Kalman_utils 

This module implements all the tracking logic of the program. It is based on 

operation of Kalman filters described in  Appendix B  to try to predict 
the future positions of the detections and thus assign them the corresponding id. 
Specifically, it has functions to: 

• Initialize the data structure to be used, which will be a dictionary with 


the identifiers as keys, and for each identified, among others, the last coordinate, the position history, its own Kalman filter, the 
predicted future position and the history of predictions  will be measured  .

• From the identifiers and a list of detections, create the assignments 

corresponding. To do this, create a distance matrix and assign 
previous identifiers to the detections that are closest and within a certain 
threshold. If there are excess detections, new 
identifiers will be created. 

• Update missed detections so that if there is an identifier that is not 

has been assigned a new detection, its new coordinate is considered the 
previous prediction, to see if in the next iteration it can be recovered. If 
it takes 10  frames  without having any real detection assigned, it is 
considered lost, since the predictions are no longer realistic.
